ABOUT THE PLAY

The Owl Girl is anarchist, pacifist, feminist, a full-length piece of magical realism, inspired by the situation in Israel/Palestine, (as well as by the issue of borders and who-stole-whose-house all over the world.) 
​The Owl Girl has been developed at the University of Utah: Boston Center for the Arts; Theatre Kavanah, Burlington Vermont; Cleveland Public Theater; Central Square Theater; Golden Thread Theater Reading Series (San Francisco) 2011; Georgetown Theater, (DC); Inkubator Festival, (DC); Castillo Theater (NYC) 2009; “Little Festival of the Unexpected,” Portland, ME; Artists of Tomorrow Festival, NYC; Smith College New Play Reading Series.
It has won prizes from the Clauder Competition (Portland Stage), Peacewriting, the Castillo Theater, and the Jewish Plays Project, and was nominated for the Susan Smith Blackburn Prize (for best play in English by a woman). In 2019, it was produced as part of a residency at the Theater for West Park, Manhattan for "exemplary and boundary pushing theater artists."

Torange Yeghiazarian, Artistic Director, Golden Thread Theater

“We presented a staged reading of The Owl Girl followed by community panel discussion on the Israeli-Palestinian conflict. While many playwrights have tackled this topic, The Owl Girl treats it with imagination, tenderness and respect…The Owl Girl is a play that should be produced across the US, theatre audiences deserve to see it.”
 

​Dick Bennett, Peacewriting Award Citation

“The fundamental fantasy and elementary, recurrent hope is the story of a Palestinian family revisiting their old home now occupied by an Israeli family, and the two families connect.   The individuals of each family play out their own fantasies, some leading to death, some to transformation…But this summary fails to suggest the new microcosm of incongruous, startling growth and change created in the house of enemy families, for those who are able.  PeaceWriting gives this award with a yearning we share with the author that Palestinians and Israelis—and ethnic and religious and political enemies throughout the world—will abandon the old confinements, and learn to fly.”
